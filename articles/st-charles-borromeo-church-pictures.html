The neighborhood down what is now Powstańców Śl. street was developed as a fashionable district beginning around 1900.   But in the early years, there was still what looks like a farmyard in the place where today there’s a bus stop and a few shops.

Clearly, the neighborhood was attractive and rather posh, with quite large houses described in records as ‘villas.’

There was a tramline in Gajowicka street that turned into Krucza street away from the church.  Local residents say that after the Second World War,  the line was used to transport rubble from bombed areas of the city to the outskirts of the city. The tracks are still visible in the street today.

The Church and Neighborhood after World War II

There are only a few published pictures of the area after the Second World War. They show Krucza Street largely in ruins from the church up to what is now Powstańców Śl. street. But residents who remember that time describe the area to the west of the church (across Gajowicka street) as a “wilderness” with extensive destruction. The pictures below were mostly shared on a website archive of historic drawings, photograps and maps of the Dolny Śląsk region.

The slow rebuilding of the neighborhood in the 1950s-1970s kept pace with the reconstruction of the church. Before the new roof was put on in the 1980s, with copper replacing the original tiles, the main steeple on the east end was crowned by a metal weathervane in the shape of a rooster. When the steeple was clad in copper, the rooster was removed and placed on the roof of the friary, where it still is today.

17. Renovation of the steeple.

18. Rear view of the friary; note the rooster on the top.

 

By the early 1980s, the church looked – at least from the outside – much as it looks today. The friary had undergone many changes, including the building of a new wing behind the original house, for parish classrooms, meeting rooms and housing for the priests.

Work on the church continues today. Section by section the outer walls are being professionally cleaned and repaired, allowing us to see more or less how the church might have looked when it was first built (minus such details as the clock on the east tower, for example, visible in pictures 3, 7 and 8 above). The stone is a beautiful honey-gold color (compare with picture 3), giving the building a much lighter appearance than it has had since at least the Second World War.

22. The Church viewed from SkyTower, 2011
Lower left: friary and parish meeting rooms

Residents of Breslau/Wrocław have always considered Saint Charles Borromeo Parish Church to be an architectural treasure, and continue to photograph the building from all angles. Even before nearby SkyTower was opened in 2012, it was obvious to photographers that St Charles Church is one of the most beautiful landmarks visible from the skyscraper, and “aerial” photos of the church are sure to be popular with visitors to the skyscraper’s observation deck.
